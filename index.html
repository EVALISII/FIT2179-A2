The Hidden Crisis

Understanding Mental Health Services Across Australia

Mental health is one of Australia's most pressing public health challenges. Every year, millions of Australians access mental health services through Medicare, yet emergency departments continue to see record numbers of crisis presentations. This visualisation explores a decade of data, who is seeking help, how the system is responding, and where the gaps remain.

Part 1, The Scale of the Problem

A decade of growing demand

The number of Australians accessing Medicare mental health services has grown by over 30% since 2014, reaching 2.7 million patients in 2023–24. Demand surged sharply during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020–21, when the national rate hit a record 112 patients per 1,000 people. Since then, rates have plateaued, suggesting the system may be approaching capacity rather than demand falling.

Where is the burden highest?

Mental health service access is not evenly distributed across Australia. Victoria records the highest patient rate per capita despite not having the largest population. The Northern Territory has the lowest rate at just 56 per 1,000, less than half the national average, likely reflecting geographic barriers, workforce shortages, and limited access in remote communities.

Which states have the lowest access per capita?

Every state except the Northern Territory sits within a narrow band of 92–109 patients per 1,000 population. The NT's rate of 56 per 1,000 is a structural outlier, not a statistical anomaly. Geographic isolation, a predominantly Indigenous population with historically lower rates of Medicare engagement, chronic workforce shortages in remote areas, and cultural barriers to Western mental health services all contribute to this persistent gap. The NT also has the highest ED mental health presentation rate of any jurisdiction, suggesting people reach crisis point before accessing any care.

Part 2, Who is Seeking Help?

Which age groups have driven the decade of growth?

Young Australians aged 18–24 have seen the steepest rise in mental health service use over the past decade, reaching rates nearly double those of older age groups by 2020–21. The 12–17 age group also shows significant growth, raising concerns about youth mental health. The 35–44 group, often managing career and family pressures simultaneously, has maintained consistently high rates across all years.

The gender gap in help-seeking

Women access Medicare mental health services at significantly higher rates than men across all working-age groups. The gap is most pronounced at ages 18–24, where women access services at nearly double the rate of men. This does not necessarily mean women have worse mental health outcomes, it likely reflects a well-documented pattern where men are less likely to seek professional help, potentially leading to more severe presentations when crises do occur.

Part 3, How is the System Responding?

Which providers are Australians turning to?

General practitioners remain the dominant entry point for mental health care, seeing far more patients than any other provider type. However, psychologists have grown the fastest over the decade, reflecting the expansion of the Better Access initiative. Psychiatrists see fewer patients overall but provide the most intensive ongoing care.

Part 4, Where the System is Failing

More services, more emergencies

Both indexed to 2014-15 as the baseline (value of 100), this chart shows the relative growth of Medicare mental health patients and emergency department presentations over the decade. Medicare patient numbers grew by around 31% by 2023-24, while ED presentations grew by around 22%. Crucially, both trends moved upward together rather than ED falling as Medicare services expanded. This suggests expanded outpatient care has not reduced crisis-level demand on emergency departments.

Does higher Medicare access mean fewer ED crises?

Not necessarily. The scatter plot below reveals a striking outlier, the Northern Territory has the lowest Medicare access rate of any state or territory, yet by far the highest ED presentation rate at 26.7 per 1,000. This suggests that where community mental health services are scarce, people reach crisis point before receiving any help at all. The remaining states cluster together with no clear relationship between Medicare access and ED presentations.

The urgency is escalating

Not only are more people presenting to emergency departments, they are arriving in more critical condition. The proportion of the most urgent triage categories has grown steadily since 2014. In 2023–24, resuscitation-level mental health presentations reached over 6,000, more than triple the 2014 figure of 1,995. This points to a system where people are not getting help early enough, resulting in more severe crises requiring emergency intervention.

Which states saw ED crises worsen after COVID?

Queensland was the only state to see a meaningful increase in ED mental health presentation rates between pre-COVID (2019–20) and 2023–24. South Australia saw the sharpest improvement. Most states returned close to pre-COVID levels, suggesting the pandemic's ED impact was temporary rather than structural in most jurisdictions, though the NT remains a persistent outlier.

What conditions drive people to emergency departments?

Mood disorders, including depression and bipolar disorder, account for the largest share of mental health ED presentations, followed closely by anxiety and stress-related conditions. Schizophrenia and psychosis-related disorders generate a disproportionately high ED burden due to the acute nature of psychotic episodes. Substance use disorders also feature prominently, reflecting the strong link between mental health and drug and alcohol conditions.

Part 5, Patterns in Community Care

Which diagnoses define community mental health care?

In community mental health settings, schizophrenia and psychosis-related disorders dominate contact volumes across all adult age groups, particularly for those aged 25–54. This reflects the chronic, relapsing nature of psychotic conditions that require sustained community support. Mood disorders are most prevalent among younger adults aged 18–34, while substance use disorders generate significant contact volumes among 18–44 year olds, highlighting the overlap between mental health and addiction services. Australia's mental health system is growing, but the data tells a more complex story. Record Medicare services have not reduced emergency department crises. Young Australians and women are disproportionately seeking help, yet men remain underserved. The Northern Territory faces a structural access gap that no other state experiences. The hidden crisis is not just the prevalence of mental illness, it is the persistent gap between what the system provides and what Australians need.
